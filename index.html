RUKA-v2
Tendon Driven Open-Source Dexterous Hand with Wrist and Abduction for Robot Learning

We present RUKA-v2, a fully open-sourced, tendon-driven humanoid hand that includes a decoupled parallel wrist and abduction/adduction capabilities on the fingers, enabling manipulation in confined environments such as cabinets and dexterous motions such as grasping thin objects and calligraphy.

Abstract

Achieving human-like dexterity in robots has been a long-standing goal in the robotics community. Last year, we released RUKA, a fully open-sourced, tendon-driven humanoid hand with 11 degrees of freedom — 2 per finger and 3 at the thumb — buildable for under $1,300. It introduced a novel data-driven approach to finger control that captures tendon dynamics within the control system.

While impressive, RUKA lacked two significant degrees of freedom essential for imitating human behavior: wrist mobility and finger abduction/adduction. In this paper, we introduce RUKA-v2: a fully open-sourced, tendon-driven humanoid hand featuring a decoupled 2-DOF parallel wrist and abduction/adduction at the fingers. The parallel wrist adds smooth, independent flexion/extension and radial/ulnar deviation, enabling manipulation in confined environments such as cabinets. Abduction enables motions such as grasping thin objects, in-hand rotation, and calligraphy.

We describe the design components of RUKA-v2, compare it to RUKA across user experiments, and demonstrate its applications for robot learning: bimanual and single-arm teleoperation on dexterous tasks and learning of autonomous policies. All 3D printer files, assembly instructions, controller software, and robot videos are open-sourced.

Overview

RUKA-v2 is a fully open-sourced, tendon-driven humanoid hand with 18 degrees of freedom, extending RUKA with a decoupled 2-DOF parallel wrist and finger abduction/adduction. All structural components are 3D printed; bearings, fasteners, and springs are off-the-shelf. Actuators are mounted proximally in the forearm to reduce distal inertia, with tendons routed through the wrist to each finger via guides that prevent sharp bends across all wrist poses.

The wrist enables manipulation in confined spaces such as cabinets, while abduction/adduction supports grasping thin objects, in-hand reorientation, and fine dexterous tasks like calligraphy. RUKA-v2 is designed to be accessible (under $3,000, no specialized tooling beyond a 3D printer), reliable (thermally stable for extended runs, easily repairable in-house), and directly suitable for robot learning via teleoperation and policy training.

Hardware Design

Building on RUKA, RUKA-v2 introduces two hardware capabilities critical for human-like manipulation: a decoupled 2-DOF parallel wrist and controlled finger abduction/adduction. All major structural components are 3D printed; bearings, fasteners, and springs are off-the-shelf parts. The total material cost is under $3,000, with actuators placed proximally in the forearm to reduce distal inertia and simplify maintenance.

RUKA-v2 hardware overview. (A) 18 degrees of freedom labeled across all joints. (B) The independent knuckle module enables abduction/adduction; the wrist module allows near-human wrist movements.

Wrist Kinematics

RUKA-v2 features a decoupled parallel wrist providing flexion/extension and radial/ulnar deviation, independently actuated while maintaining predictable tendon geometry. Intersecting rotation axes meet at a single pivot point defined by a passive spherical ball joint, minimizing translational palm motion during rotation and reducing cross-axis coupling.

Finger tendons pass through a through-hole near the wrist rotation center, then are redirected by a bearing-supported routing plate — limiting wrist-induced variation in tendon length and improving controllability across all wrist poses.

Finger Abduction / Adduction

RUKA-v2 adds controlled abduction/adduction at the MCP joints via independent knuckle modules, enabling lateral finger spacing for pinching thin objects, conforming to irregular geometry, and stabilizing objects during in-hand reorientation.

Each joint is driven by a dedicated forearm tendon, with a spring return to neutral. The middle finger remains fixed as a stable geometric reference. This configuration adds passive compliance during contact and enforces a well-defined default posture.

Optional DIP / PIP Coupling

RUKA-v2 offers an optional rigid DIP/PIP coupling mechanism that enforces synchronized motion between the distal and proximal interphalangeal joints via fixed-length coupling strings routed through internal channels.

This improves repeatability and simplifies control compared to RUKA’s passive coupling, at the cost of per-joint compliance. The coupling is provided as an add-on with full assembly instructions.

Controller

RUKA-v2 uses Anyteleop's vector based retargeting and a linear interpolation controller to determine motor commands from hand keypoints.
